Based on the sources, particularly the guide from *AnalystAI*, the **"Splicing"
trick** (also referred to as the "Multi-Step Assembly" method) is the most reliable
way to force Suno to create a song with two contradictory genres (e.g., switching
from dark industrial rap to bright indie pop) that refuses to blend into a "mush"
during a single generation.
Here is the step-by-step workflow to execute this trick:
### 1. Generate the First Half (Genre A)
Instead of trying to prompt the whole song at once, focus entirely on the first
genre.
* **Style Prompt:** Describe **only** the first genre (e.g., "Dark electronic hip
hop, aggressive, distorted synth bass").
* **Lyrics:** Input only the `[Intro]` and `[Verse 1]`. Do not include the chorus
yet.
* **Goal:** Generate clips until you find one that ends cleanly or at a moment of
high tension suitable for a switch.
### 2. The "Extend" Switch
Once you have the perfect Verse 1 clip:
* **Action:** Click **Extend** on that specific clip.
* **Time:** Set the extension to start exactly where the verse ends.
* **Clear the Settings:** **Delete everything** in the Style of Music box.
### 3. Generate the Second Half (Genre B)
Now, input the completely new genre information for the extension.
* **New Style Prompt:** Describe the **opposing** genre (e.g., "Upbeat indie pop,
bright piano rock, happy, major key").
* **Lyrics:** Start the lyrics immediately with the `[Chorus]` tag.
* **Result:** Suno will take the audio "tail" of the aggressive dark verse and
attempt to morph it into the bright pop chorus. Because it treats the extension as a
new generation context, it adheres to the new style prompt while maintaining the
tempo/key continuity of the previous audio file.
### 4. Why This Works
The AI struggles to hold two conflicting concepts (Dark vs. Happy) in its "latent
space" simultaneously without blending them. By splitting the generation, you force
the model to commit 100% of its resources to Genre A, and then force it to pivot
100% to Genre B during the extension, creating a distinct "hard cut" or radical
transition similar to songs like *Bohemian Rhapsody* or *Ode to Sleep*,,.

DOCUMENT: How can I use Metric Modulation for odd time signatures.pdf
========================================

Based on the provided sources, specifically the Reddit findings by user *Jmin158*
and the *Architectural Framework* analysis, using **Metric Modulation** is a
workaround to "trick" Suno into generating odd time signatures (like 5/4 or 7/8)
because the model defaults heavily to 4/4 time.
Here is how to implement Metric Modulation in your prompt:
### 1. The Concept: "Tricking the Grid"
Suno does not natively understand commands like "5/4 time." Instead of asking for
the time signature directly, you change the **BPM (Tempo)** and command the AI to
play in **Tuplets** (e.g., Quintuplets for 5/4). This forces a specific grouping of
notes that mathematically aligns with an odd meter.
### 2. The Formula
To calculate the necessary BPM change, use this formula:
$$BPM_{modulated} = \left( \frac{\text{Base BPM}}{\text{Tuplet Division}} \right)
\times 4$$
* **Base BPM:** The starting tempo of your track (e.g., 120 BPM).
* **Tuplet Division:** The odd number you want (e.g., **5** for 5/4, **7** for
7/8).
* **4:** The standard meter base (quarter notes).
**Example Calculation for 5/4 Time:**
* **Start:** 120 BPM.
* **Target:** 5/4 feel.
* **Math:** $(120 / 5) \times 4 = 96$.
* **Result:** You must set the tempo to **96 BPM** for that section.
### 3. The Prompt Syntax
You cannot just type the BPM; you must explain the modulation in the prompt so the
AI contextually understands the shift. Use specific terms like "QUINTUPLET" or
"SEPTUPLET" rather than "5/4".
**Copy-Paste Template:**
> **[Transition] (Tempo SLOW (96 BPM), 4/4 time. Metric Modulation: This tempo is
derived from 120 BPM by a precise 5:4 Quintuplet Modulation.)**
**Detailed Example from Source:**
> **[Slow Soothing Instrumental Intro]** **(Tempo SLOW (76.8 BPM), 4/4 time. Metric
Modulation: This tempo is derived from 128 BPM by a precise 3:5 Modulation.)**
### 4. Why This Works
By explicitly stating the mathematical derivation ("derived from 120 BPM"), you
prevent Suno from getting "confused". The model interprets "Quintuplet" as a
performance instruction to group notes in fives. When played at the modulated
(slower) BPM, these groups of five take up the same amount of time as a standard 4/4
bar at the original speed, effectively creating a 5/4 feel.

Based on the provided sources, using **Audio Seeds** (uploading audio) is widely
considered the most reliable method for forcing Suno to generate music in odd time
signatures (like 5/4, 7/8, or complex polymeters), as the model’s text-to-audio
engine defaults heavily to 4/4 time.
Here is the step-by-step process for using Audio Seeds to achieve odd meters.
### 1. Create or Find Your "Rhythmic Seed"
Suno needs a reference audio file that clearly establishes the odd meter. The AI
will analyze the transient peaks (the beats) of this file and attempt to mimic that
grid.
* **Record a Riff:** You do not need to be a professional musician. You can simply
record yourself clapping, beatboxing, or playing a simple riff on a guitar or piano
in the desired time signature (e.g., counting *1-2-3-1-2* for 5/4).
* **Use MIDI or Loops:** If you cannot play an instrument, search for
"royalty-free 5/4 drum loops" or use a DAW (Digital Audio Workstation) to program a
simple MIDI beat in the desired time signature. Export this as an MP3 or WAV.
* **Keep it Simple:** The seed does not need to be complex; it just needs a clear,
repetitive rhythm that "locks" the AI into the groove.
### 2. Upload and Configure
Once you have your audio file (clip), follow these steps in the Suno interface:
* **Upload the Audio:** Use the **Upload Audio** feature to import your rhythmic
seed into Suno.
* **Select "Extend" or "Cover":**
* **Extend:** If you want the song to *start* with your audio and continue,
use **Extend**.
* **Cover:** If you want to transform your uploaded melody/rhythm into a
different genre while keeping the time signature, use the **Cover** feature (if
available in your specific interface version/tier).
### 3. Adjust the "Audio Influence" Slider
This is the critical setting. The **Audio Influence** slider determines how strictly
Suno adheres to the timing and structure of your upload versus its own training
(which wants to revert to 4/4).
* **The Sweet Spot:** Set Audio Influence between **60% and 75%** for a "featured
upload" where the AI tracks your rhythm closely but applies new instrumentation.
* **Too Low (<40%):** The AI may interpret your odd meter as "messy" 4/4 and
correct it back to a standard beat.
* **Too High (>80%):** The AI might simply copy your upload without adding enough
creative variation or "hallucinating" the rest of the track effectively.
### 4. Prompting the Style
Even though the audio seed dictates the *rhythm*, you must still use the **Style of
Music** prompt to dictate the *texture*.
* **Combine Inputs:** After uploading your 7/8 guitar riff, you can prompt for

*your* 7/8 grid.
* **Reinforce with Text:** It helps to reinforce the audio seed with text tags
like `[Math Rock]` or `[Irregular Meter]` in the style prompt to ensure the AI
"understands" the context of the audio seed.

1. **Record/Find** a 10–30 second clip of the odd rhythm (claps, drums, or riff).

Based on the provided sources, here are the **instrument meta tags and formatting
behaviors** that Suno AI commonly ignores or misinterprets during delivery.
### 1. Negative Instrument Tags ("The Pink Elephant Paradox")
Suno has significant difficulty processing "negative" constraints regarding
instruments. It often ignores instructions to *exclude* specific sounds because it
focuses on the keywords present in the prompt.
* **"No Drums" / "No Piano":** Users frequently report that explicitly typing "No
piano" or "exclude guitar" results in the AI *adding* those instruments because it
sees the words "piano" or "guitar" in the token list.
* **"Solo Instrument" Exclusions:** Attempts to force a solo by excluding all
other instruments (e.g., "Guitar solo only, exclude drums/bass") often fail, with
the AI defaulting to a full band arrangement.
### 2. Complex & "Overstuffed" Tags
Suno's model struggles with long, descriptive chains within a single set of
brackets. These are often treated as noise or, worse, **sung as lyrics**.
* **Multi-Adjective Chains:** Tags like `[Soaring Guitar Solo with Divebomb]` or
`[Layered Crescendo]` are frequently ignored or misinterpreted. The model prefers
simple, standard terms like `[Guitar Solo]`.
* **"Kitchen Sink" Lists:** Listing too many instruments (e.g., "Violin, cello,
trumpet, sax, piano, synth, drums") causes the AI to "hallucinate a mush of sound"
or simply ignore half the list to maintain a coherent mix.
### 3. Plural vs. Singular Nouns
While not strictly "ignored," using plural nouns can lead to muddy or indistinct
results compared to singular nouns.
* **"Guitars" vs. "Guitar":** Sources recommend using singular terms like "Guitar"
or "String Section" rather than "Guitars" or "Strings." The plural form can confuse
the AI into generating a "wall of noise" rather than distinct instrument lines.
### 4. Technical Time Signatures & "Fake" Quality Tags
Suno ignores technical constraints that do not map to its audio training data.
* **Time Signatures:** Tags like `[3/4 Time]`, `[5/4]`, or `[6/8 Time]` are
generally ignored as the model defaults heavily to 4/4 time. Workarounds like
"Waltz" (for 3/4) are necessary.
* **Quality Boosters:** Keywords like `[4k]`, `[Masterpiece]`, `[Award-winning]`,
or `[Trending on ArtStation]` are treated as "creative noise" and ignored. The AI
responds better to production terms like `[High Fidelity]` or `[Wide Stereo]`.
### 5. Niche Sound Effects (Context Dependent)
While Suno can generate sound effects, specific tags are often ignored if they don't
fit the musical context.
* **Specific Foley:** Tags like `[Barking]`, `[Door Slam]`, or `[Phone Ringing]`
are unreliable. They work best when they fit the "scene" (e.g., an intro), but are
often ignored in the middle of a dense arrangement.
